## Overview

I led the evolution of the Tableau Design System from an externally produced PDF style guide into a centralized, operational design system that supported both design and engineering teams across the organization. What began as a static visual language initiative evolved into foundational product infrastructure that enabled shared UI standards, governance, cross-functional collaboration, and scalable product development practices.

My role focused on defining the operational strategy behind the system, building organizational alignment, establishing adoption pathways, and creating the infrastructure necessary for long-term sustainability. Over time, the work expanded beyond visual consistency into governance, enablement, platform modernization, and foundational token architecture.

## The Challenge

When I joined the initiative, the “design system” primarily existed as a PDF style guide created by an external agency. The effort focused heavily on visual language and brand consistency, but lacked the operational and technical foundations required to scale across a complex product organization.

Several critical gaps existed:

- No centralized source of truth for designers or engineers
- No shared component libraries or reusable implementation standards
- No governance model or contribution process
- No alignment between design and engineering workflows
- More than 50 unsanctioned UI libraries existed across product teams
- Teams operated independently with inconsistent UX patterns and duplicated effort
- Long-term scalability, adoption strategy, and operational sustainability had not yet been considered

Accessibility was also notably absent from the original initiative. Early efforts centered primarily on visual consistency and brand expression rather than inclusive design standards or implementation guidance. As the system matured, it became increasingly clear that accessibility needed to be treated as a foundational systems concern rather than a downstream product responsibility.

The challenge was not simply creating UI assets. The larger challenge was building organizational trust, aligning stakeholders, and creating a system teams would voluntarily adopt because it improved velocity, consistency, collaboration, and maintainability.

## My Opportunity

### Establishing the Foundation

I began by conducting a comprehensive audit of the organization’s existing ecosystem to understand both operational and technical gaps. This included evaluating:

- Existing UI libraries and product inconsistencies
- Current workflows and tooling limitations
- Stakeholders across design, engineering, and product
- Potential consumers of the system and their needs
- Missing governance, scalability, and enablement capabilities

This discovery phase helped reposition the design system from a branding exercise into a scalable product platform initiative.

### Creating Centralized Resources

To support adoption, I helped establish a centralized ecosystem of resources that gave teams clear entry points into the system. This included:

- Shared UI kits
- Documentation and usage guidance
- Centralized code repositories
- Governance models and contribution pathways
- Support and enablement programs for product teams

Rather than positioning the system as a top-down mandate, I focused on creating enablement structures that made adoption easier, more collaborative, and operationally valuable for teams.

### Recognizing Accessibility Gaps

As the system evolved, I recognized that accessibility had not been deeply embedded into the original framework. While we improved consistency and operational maturity, accessibility standards and implementation guidance remained underdeveloped across both design and engineering workflows.

This realization influenced later conversations around governance, tokenization, and long-term scalability. It reinforced the importance of treating accessibility as foundational infrastructure rather than an enhancement layered on after adoption.

### Driving Adoption Through Education and Partnership

Adoption was initially slow. Many teams already had established workflows and custom UI libraries. Instead of forcing standardization, I focused on building alignment through education, transparency, and partnership.

I hosted open forums with both designers and engineers to discuss:

- The operational cost of fragmented UI systems
- The relationship between consistency and product velocity
- Shared ownership between design and engineering
- Long-term maintenance and scalability considerations

These conversations helped shift the perception of the design system from a design-led initiative into a cross-functional business investment.

### Building the Ambassador Program

To strengthen alignment with product teams, I established the Design System Ambassador Program, which created direct partnerships between the design system team and embedded product teams.

Ambassadors:

- Created feedback loops between teams
- Increased visibility into adoption blockers and product needs
- Helped socialize roadmap priorities
- Encouraged shared ownership across the organization

The program significantly improved communication, trust, and organizational participation in the system’s evolution.

### Leading Tooling Transformation

I also led the organization’s transition from Sketch to Figma. This migration represented more than a tooling change. It became an opportunity to modernize collaboration workflows, improve library distribution, and establish a more scalable operational model for shared design assets.

The transition enabled:

- Better cross-functional collaboration
- More reliable library management
- Faster updates and distribution
- Improved accessibility to shared resources

### Establishing the First Token Architecture

One of my final initiatives was establishing the organization’s first design token library. This work laid the groundwork for a more scalable and maintainable system architecture across products and platforms.

The token framework created the foundation for:

- Shared visual variables across products
- Scalable theming and future platform flexibility
- Better alignment between design and engineering systems
- More maintainable UI infrastructure
- Future opportunities for semantic color mapping and accessibility improvements

This marked an important shift from static style guidance toward systems-driven platform thinking.

## Outcome

- Consolidated more than 50 unsanctioned UI libraries into a centralized system
- Established the organization’s first formal governance and enablement model for design systems
- Created centralized tooling, documentation, and support resources for design and engineering teams
- Improved cross-functional collaboration through open forums and ambassador partnerships
- Led the organization-wide migration from Sketch to Figma
- Established the company’s first design token architecture to support long-term scalability
- Increased organizational visibility and alignment around design system adoption and operational maturity
- Helped reposition the design system from a visual language initiative into a strategic product infrastructure investment

## Reflections

One of the biggest lessons from this work was that design systems succeed through organizational alignment as much as through components and tooling. Early on, it became clear that consistency alone was not a compelling enough reason for adoption. Teams embraced the system when they saw operational value through reduced duplication, increased velocity, and stronger collaboration.

I also learned the importance of investing in governance and enablement early. Creating assets without support structures inevitably leads to fragmentation over time. The ambassador program, open forums, and centralized support resources became just as important as the visual system itself.

Another important lesson was recognizing how easily accessibility can become deprioritized when organizations are primarily focused on consistency, tooling, and operational alignment. While the system successfully unified fragmented UI efforts, accessibility should have been embedded as a foundational requirement much earlier in the process.

In hindsight, I would have pushed earlier for accessibility standards, semantic guidance, and inclusive interaction patterns alongside the visual language foundation. Establishing accessibility as a core systems principle sooner would have improved implementation consistency and reduced downstream remediation efforts across product teams.

Looking back, I also would have accelerated the development of shared interaction patterns and implementation standards alongside the visual system. While establishing foundational consistency was necessary, expanding earlier into scalable engineering and interaction frameworks could have reduced downstream inconsistencies and accelerated adoption across teams.
